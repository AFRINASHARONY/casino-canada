History of Lottery in Canada

Like most countries around the world, the purpose of the lottery is to fundraise for public services, such as building roads and bridges, sewage systems, and bin collection. Indeed 1976, the lottery helped raise money to host the 1976 Olympics in Montreal.

The lottery is thought to have been around for the best part of the 17th century in Canada, becoming more structured throughout the 1800s until the Canadian government decided to step in and ban the practice in 1875, which was shortly followed by a prohibition of all forms of gambling by 1892 as part of the Canadian Criminal Code.

Fewer than 100 years later, the Criminal Code was amended to allow lotteries to run once more for the funding of special nationwide projects both provincially and federally. In 1974 the first national lottery in Canada was held in what was referred to as the Canada Olympic Lottery, named as such because it helped raise money to host the 1976 Olympics in Montreal.

In the modern era, Canadians can play three national lottery games - Lotto 6/49, Lotto Max, and the Daily Grand.

Types of Lottery Games Available

While there are three main national games, the Canada lottery landscape is vast. There are plenty of lotteries in Canada - here are a few examples:

  • Lotto Max

    This is a popular national lotto in Canada, with draws on Fridays that offer prizes of up to $60 million. Players choose seven numbers from 1 to 50 for each ticket.

  • Lotto 6/49

    This lottery game offers players the chance to win a progressive jackpot by selecting six numbers from 1 to 49 and a bonus ball.

  • Daily Grand

    This Canadian lottery is offered by the Interprovincial Lottery Corporation, and features draws twice a week. Players select five different numbers from 1 to 25, as well as one grand number between 1 and 5. The winner bags a jackpot.

  • Atlantic 49

    Players have the chance to win up to $2 million every Wednesday and Saturday. They choose six different numbers from 1 to 49 for each ticket, as well as a Bonus number between 1 and 49.

  • Ontario Encore

    This lottery is available exclusively in the province of Ontario, and features draws seven days a week. Players select seven numbers from 0 to 9 for each ticket they purchase, with prizes up to $1,000,000 available. Unlike other Canadian lottos, the Ontario Encore numbers must match the exact sequence on the lotto ticket. For example, numbers 1, 9, 0, 4, 4, 0, 9 would only pay the jackpot if the numbers came out in that exact order.

Lottery Prizes & Payouts

The prize can vary significantly from a few dollars to millions. For example, popular lottery games such as LOTTO 6/49 and Lotto Max offer jackpots that start at $5 million and can go up to over $60 million. In addition, there are unique draws such as Super 7, which has seen an enormous payout of more than $50 million!

Progressive jackpots are also popular in Canadian lotteries. These increase every time the lottery is drawn without a winner, so it is impossible to put a cap on the maximum amount of money that can be won.

While the odds of winning a national lottery are monumental, there are still plenty of examples of players striking it big to win incredible money.

There are some national statistics backing up the popularity of the lotto. For example, in 2018, Canadians spent over $7 billion on lottery tickets - that's a number that would have only increased over the past five years.

In those same figures, it was revealed the Atlantic Lottery Corporation (ALC) was the largest lottery corporation in Canada, with sales of $2.2 billion, while the the Ontario Lottery and Gaming Corporation (OLG) is next highest, with sales of $2.1 billion in the same year.

Some lottery tickets in Canada are only valid for selected jurisdictions - make sure to check the terms and conditions and rules of each lotto ticket to get all the information.

Lottery Regulations & Law

The Canadian lottery is regulated by the Interprovincial Lottery Corporation (ILC), an organization that oversees lottery operations in all Canadian provinces and territories. Each jurisdiction has its own set of rules for those who play lottery online or via other means, but there are some common regulations.

To play a Canadian lottery game, players must be at least 18 years old and purchase a ticket from an authorised retailer. Tickets can also be purchased online in some areas. In most provinces, players are allowed to purchase tickets for multiple draws at one time.

Generally, prizes must be claimed within a certain period of time and may require the winner to present valid identification and proof of purchase. The ILC also has regulations in place for winners who choose to remain anonymous.

Lottery Scams & Fraud

Unfortunately, there are some examples of lottery scams and fraudulent behaviour by criminals within the Canada lottery online and land-based sphere.

Some examples include:

  • A criminal contacts a player, claiming they have won a large sum of money with the promise of sending winnings if they provide personal information or pay fees.
  • Another common form of lottery fraud in Canada involves people selling fraudulent tickets online or through the mail. These tickets are usually fake and the person selling them is not affiliated with any official lottery organisation.
  • There have also been instances of representatives claiming to be from legitimate lotteries, but collecting personal information or asking for payment before sending a prize, which is never forthcoming.

Responsible Gambling Practices

There are several steps players can take to avoid the risks of gambling addiction from playing the lottery online in Canada.

They include:

  1. Set a budget and stick to it: Before playing the lotto online, players should set a budget they can afford to lose without any adverse financial consequences.
  2. Take regular breaks from gambling: It can help reduce excessive spending and addictive behavior.
  3. Treat lotto betting as a form of entertainment: While it is possible to make money playing the lottery, the odds are stacked against the player.
